About me

short self-bio
+ interests

Hi there! My name is David Fan and I'm currently a student at Princeton University. For as long as I can remember, I've been enamored by all things science/math/computer flavored, and I'm excited to continue discovering new things over the next few years.

In summer 2016, I hope to complete an internship in research, data analytics, web development or software engineering. My goal is to exit summer 2016 with valuable coding experience as well as perspective on either the software industry or research in computational biology/biotech/biomedical sciences.

More broadly, my mission is to create value in the world . I want to leverage my skills and talents in a manner that's most fruitful for others and myself, and in the process do meaningful work. From my experience so far, I believe I can do so through work at the intersection between computing and other disciplines.

Research-wise, currently I'm interested in all the little mysteries hidden in living systems that can be decoded through computers and big data analytics. I believe that work done at the intersection between computer science, biology and many other disciplines can potentially rewire the way in which we look at medicine. I'm also interested in energy and materials science with relevance to biofuels, catalysis and energy storage.

Summer 2014

Rutgers New Jersey Medical School - Partners in Science Program

Paid Genetics Research Intern

Researched genetic mechanisms of aging in Drosophila fruit flies, specifically the effects of the Ras•GTP-Raf-MEK-ERK signaling pathway on organismal and organ senescence. Conducted lifespan and stress (starvation, oxidative and heat) assays on flies with transgenes expressing varied levels of Rpd3 protein in the heart tissue. I also conducted heartbeat measurements on flies throughout various stages in their lifespan to analyze heart-function decline with age. Coauthor of paper published in Aging.

Was a 2015 Intel Science Talent Search (STS) Semifinalist with my work.

Summer 2013

Rutgers University Dept. of Physics - Partners in Science Program

Paid Physics Research Intern

Explored symmetry-breaking phase transitions in multiferroic materials (i.e. rare-earth hexagonal manganites). Polished and depth-profiled these materials to image their topological defect distribution. This work led to a paper published in Nature Physics that I coauthored.

As a side project I also conducted high-temperature resistivity measurements on novel rare-earth copper titanates using a two-probe method to characterize their ferroelectric transition temperatures.
