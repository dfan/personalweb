About me

short self-bio
+ interests

Hi there! My name is David Fan and I am a Computer Science student at Princeton University who is passionate about using technology and quantitative methods to solve interdisciplinary problems.

In summer 2018, I hope to do a software engineering internship in the Bay Area, as I have never visited the West Coast. My goal is to exit summer 2018 with additional experience writing production quality code and shipping features from start to finish.

Last summer, I was a software engineering intern at Phosphorus, a NYC based Series-A startup in the clinical genomics space. The previous summer, I did bioinformatics research at Harvard Medical School through the Harvard-MIT HST Summer Institute and developed two web tools for visualizing geographic trends in insurance data and doing client-side single-cell transcriptomics analysis respectively.

On campus, I co-direct HackPrinceton, am director emeritus for the Princeton University Science Olympiad Invitational Tournament and do web development for the Princeton Undergraduate Research Journal and Princeton University Math Competition. I have experience in numerous projects and have both led and worked with large teams.

Broadly, my mission is to create value in the world . Through everything I do, I hope to utilize my skills and talents to make the world a better place. From my experiences so far, I believe I can do so best through work at the intersection between computing and big data. Please reach out if you think we might work well together.

Jun. - Aug. 2014

Rutgers New Jersey Medical School - Partners in Science Program

Researched genetic mechanisms of aging in Drosophila fruit flies, specifically the effects of the Ras•GTP-Raf-MEK-ERK signaling pathway on organismal and organ senescence. Conducted lifespan and stress (starvation, oxidative and heat) assays on flies with transgenes expressing varied levels of Rpd3 protein in the heart tissue. I also conducted heartbeat measurements on flies throughout various stages in their lifespan to analyze heart-function decline with age. Coauthor of paper published in Aging.

Was a 2015 Intel Science Talent Search (STS) Semifinalist with my work.

Jun. - Aug. 2013

Rutgers University Dept. of Physics - Partners in Science Program

Explored symmetry-breaking phase transitions in multiferroic materials (i.e. rare-earth hexagonal manganites). Polished and depth-profiled these materials to image their topological defect distribution. This work led to a paper published in Nature Physics that I coauthored.

As a side project I also conducted high-temperature resistivity measurements on novel rare-earth copper titanates using a two-probe method to characterize their ferroelectric transition temperatures.
